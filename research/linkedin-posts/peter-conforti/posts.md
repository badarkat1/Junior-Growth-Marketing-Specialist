# Post Title / Hook
Big Companies Ignore Data... Small Ones Obsess

# Full Content
One thing shocks me: Small companies are obsessed with performance data; big companies often couldn't care less. A 3,000‑person org (AlixPartners) is cynical about platform stats. They say: ‘There is a marketing culture led by data the platforms create, rather than good marketing.’ Yes, you can chase likes and impressions (e.g., dog photos). But why spend time on potentially ineffective content? Data can guide, but chasing vanity metrics won’t drive real impact. Instead, focus on consistent, authentic content that builds trust and wins hearts

# Summary: Conforti relays a discussion with a large-company marketer who warns against letting LinkedIn analytics dictate strategy. He argues that obsessing over likes/impressions is less productive than building genuine relationships through consistent content. Even low-engagement posts can gradually generate significant revenue in the background.

# Main Idea / Strategy
Quality Over Vanity Metrics: High engagement on irrelevant content (gimmicks) doesn’t equal business value; trust-building content may yield low visible metrics but real conversions (as Barney noted they got seven figures without viral posts).
Consistency and Trust: The “7 likes” post is still working; focus on steady, authentic posts that keep you present in buyers’ minds, rather than chasing short-lived trends.
Long-Term Perspective: Use metrics as signals for learning, not as ultimate goals. Content’s real payoff may only be visible months later (consistent storytelling leads to pipeline).

# Post Title / Hook
More content isn’t always the answer. In 2025, it’s depth

# Full Content
More content isn't always the answer. In 2025, it's depth. Emily Kramer spends 30–40hrs on each newsletter. Result? 51K Substack subs (bigger than her LinkedIn following). She follows a strategy: (1) Create 1 deep, original, valuable piece (unique perspective), (2) Repurpose it into 5–6 shorter LinkedIn posts, each with key insights tied to the newsletter’s theme. This reinforces her message and streamlines creation. Takeaway: Marketers over-index on short-form. You don’t have to only create snackable content. Balance long-form depth with versatile short posts to build a powerful content stack.”

#Summary: Conforti highlights Emily Kramer’s approach as a case study: investing heavy effort in one in-depth piece and then breaking it into multiple shorter posts. He suggests B2B brands can learn from this by mixing high-quality long-form content with repurposed snippets rather than simply churning out more short posts.

# Main Idea/Strategy
Deep Over Light: In an era of content saturation, creating one well-researched, original piece can outperform many quick posts.
Multi-Format Repurposing: Use long-form as a source to generate multiple shorter posts, amplifying its reach and reinforcing the message consistently.
Strategic Content Stack: A balance of “term papers” and snackable content helps stand out—don’t neglect long-form thinking (unique POV yields loyal audience).
